Chapter 5 : Decision Trees

Decision Tree

A decision tree classifier uses a structure of branching decisions, which channel examples into a final predicted class value.

Potencial Use:

. Credit scoring models in which the criteria that causes an applicant to be rejected need to be clearly documented and free from bias

. Marketing studies of customer behavior such as satisfaction or churn, which will be shared with management or advertising agencies

. Diagnosis of medical conditions based on laboratory measurements, symptoms, or the rate of disease progression

The C5.0 Decision Tree Algorithm:

Strength:

. An all-purpose classifier that does well on most problems

. Highly automatic learning process, which can handle numeric or nominal features as well as missing data

. Excludes unimportant features

Weakness:

. It is easy to overfit or underfit the model

. Decision tree models are often biased towards splits on features having a large number of levelsCan have trouble modeling some relationships due to reliance on axis-parallel splits

. Small changes in the training data can result in large changes in decision logic

. Large trees can be difficult to interpret and the decisions they make may seem counterintuitive

How To Pick Splits

Common practice is to test various splits that divide the values into groups greater than or less than a numeric threshold. This reduces the numeric feature into a two-level categorical feature that allows information gain to be calculated as usual. The numeric cut point yielding the largest information gain is chosen for the split

Pruning The Decision Tree

The process of pruning a decision tree involves reducing its size such that it generalizes better to unseen data.

Post-pruning

Is more effective. Allows the algorithm to be certain that all the important data structures were discovered.
